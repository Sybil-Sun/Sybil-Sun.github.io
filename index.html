Job Market Paper

Title: Expected Fertility, Long-Term Contracts, and the Gender Wage Gap (2025)

Abstract: This paper studies how employers’ expectations about women’s future fertility affect wages in frictional labor markets. In settings with costly separations and long-horizon tasks, firms set wages based on the present value of match productivity rather than solely on contemporaneous output; if employers expect future caregiving interruptions, they post lower wages today (statistical discrimination). Exploiting China’s relaxation of the One-Child Policy as a quasi-experiment, a difference-in-differences design shows that women’s wages fell by 15.3% immediately after the policy change, despite no short-run increase in births. To interpret these facts, I extend a Burdett–Mortensen wage-posting model with on-the-job search to include human-capital accumulation that depends on nonwork time, making wage offers long-term (present-value) objects. Estimating the model on Chinese wage distributions before and after the policy, I find that more than 40% of the post-policy widening of the gender wage gap is explained by firms’ discounting of women’s expected future productivity; in a counterfactual where female labor-market dynamism (offer arrival and separation rates) is set to male levels, the policy-induced widening falls to 10.4%. The results imply that, absent changes in household time allocation or market dynamism, employment-protection policies that raise separation costs can induce ex-ante wage markdowns for women and thus fail to narrow—and may widen—the gender wage gap.
